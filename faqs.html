Eating a healthy diet rich in fruits, vegetables, and whole grains. Maintaining a healthy weight through regular exercise and portion control. Avoiding tobacco products and limiting alcohol consumption. Protecting yourself from the sun by using sunscreen and avoiding tanning beds. Getting regular screenings for cancers such as breast, cervical, colorectal, and skin cancer.

Avoiding exposure to tobacco smoke, both firsthand and secondhand. Minimizing exposure to harmful chemicals in household products, such as cleaners and pesticides. Following safety guidelines when handling hazardous materials at work or home. Testing your home for radon, a naturally occurring gas that can increase the risk of lung cancer. Choosing organic foods and products when possible to reduce pesticide exposure.

The HPV vaccine helps protect against human papillomavirus (HPV) infection, which can lead to cervical, anal, and other cancers. The hepatitis B vaccine reduces the risk of liver cancer associated with chronic hepatitis B infection. Vaccines for hepatitis C and Epstein-Barr virus (EBV) are also being researched for their potential to prevent related cancers

While some cancers have a strong hereditary component, most are caused by a combination of genetic and environmental factors. Genetic counseling and testing can help individuals understand their inherited risk factors for certain cancers. Screening and preventive measures, such as increased surveillance or prophylactic surgery, may be recommended for individuals with a high genetic risk of cancer. It's essential to discuss genetic testing and its implications with a healthcare professional who specializes in cancer genetics.
